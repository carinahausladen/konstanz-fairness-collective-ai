# Agency, Decisions, Free Will

**Guest lecture — Thomas Müller** (philosophy, with a background in physics and mathematics; works on quantum mechanics, indeterminism, and formal models of agency). No paper presentations this week — a conceptual session.

---

## 1. Why this question sits under the whole course

Every week so far has *used* the word agent without auditing it. Gudiño's "LLMs **as Agents** for Augmented Democracy" (Week 7) lets a model act on a citizen's behalf. Lynch's "**Agentic** Misalignment" (Week 3) asks what an LLM does when it can take consequential actions. Both presuppose that the system is the kind of thing that *acts, chooses, and pursues goals* — that it is an agent, not merely a mechanism producing outputs.

Müller's lecture turns that presupposition into the question: **what would have to be true for an LLM — or a person, a collective, an animal — to genuinely count as an agent?** The payoff for this course is direct. If "agency" is just a convenient label we drape over a deterministic mechanism, then "agentic misalignment" and "augmented democracy" describe engineering problems, not moral ones. If agency is real, the moral stakes are too.

---

## 2. The core vocabulary

- **Agency** — the capacity to act, pursue goals, learn, and make choices.
- **Decision** — possibly a *special* kind of action, not just any behavior.
- **Free will** — whether actions are genuinely open, or fully fixed by the underlying physics.
- **Ontology** — the question *"what really exists?"* Do numbers exist? Causes? Decisions? Agency itself?

A recurring move in mid-20th-century philosophy is that many apparent problems are artifacts of how language is used — concepts misapplied past their home domain. (Heidegger's "*Das Nichts nichtet*" — "the Nothing nothings" — is the stock cautionary example: grammar lets us build a sentence that may name no genuine question.) The discipline of the lecture is therefore **conceptual analysis**: clarify a vague concept by isolating its core features before asking whether anything satisfies them.

---

## 3. Agency is not moral agency

The first distinction does real work for the rest of the course.

| | **Agency** | **Moral agency** |
|---|---|---|
| What it involves | acting, wanting, learning | responsibility, blame, shame, praise |
| What it requires | goal-directed behavior | standing as a bearer of obligations |

The two come apart. An animal — or an AI — might have **agency without moral agency**: it acts and learns, but we do not hold it *responsible*. This is exactly the gap that "agentic misalignment" lives in. An LLM that takes a harmful action may be an *agent* in the thin sense (it acted toward a goal) while no one thinks the model itself is *blameworthy*. Keeping the two senses separate is what stops the AI-safety conversation from sliding between "the system did something" and "the system is at fault."

Three kinds of attribution overlap here but stay distinct: **causal** (X brought about Y), **moral** (X is answerable for Y), and **agency** (X *did* Y as an act). Much confusion in the AI debate is one of these masquerading as another.

---

## 4. The reductionist challenge: is agency *real*?

The hard question is ontological. Granting that "agency" is a useful description, **is there anything in the world that the description picks out** — or is there only physics underneath, with agency a story we layer on top?

This is an old worry in new clothes. Ancient atomism held that only atoms moving in the void are *fundamentally* real; everything else is derivative. The modern version: only the physical microstate is real, and "she decided to vote" is a coarse-grained gloss on particles obeying laws. Quine framed the whole field with one question — *"What is there?"* — and the reductionist answer threatens to leave decisions off the list.

This is the load-bearing tension of the lecture: **does a complete mechanistic explanation of a system dissolve its agency, or coexist with it?**

---

## 5. Science itself presupposes agency

A striking turn: the reductionist picture, taken to erase agency, undercuts the very practice that produced it. **Science is not passive observation — it is intervention.** Experiments require *choosing* what to manipulate, *deciding* what to test, *acting* on the world to see what follows. Modern science is precisely the shift from watching nature to experimenting on it.

So agency is not one more phenomenon science might explain away; it is a **precondition of doing science at all**. A worldview that denies any room for genuine choosing cannot account for the experimenters who built it. Understanding agency turns out to be necessary for understanding science.

---

## 6. Proposed features of agency

Müller's explication offers candidate core features — what a system would need to count as an agent:

1. **Embodiment** — an agent must physically exist and be locatable. (Note the awkward fit for AI: a model's "body" may be *distributed* across data centers — "more like fungi than animals" — which strains our animal-shaped intuitions about where an agent is.)
2. **Indeterminism / an open future** — genuine alternatives must be available; there must be more than one way the future could go.
3. **Wanting / goals** — aims, preferences, reward functions; something the agent is *for*.
4. **A learning history** — the agent develops through experience over time, rather than arriving fully fixed.

Read against this list, current LLMs satisfy some features cleanly (goals-as-reward, a training history) and others only by stretching them (embodiment), while the second feature — an open future — is exactly the one determinism puts in doubt.

---

## 7. Defeaters: when agency disappears

Equally informative is the list of conditions under which we *withdraw* the attribution of agency:

- a **puppet** under direct external control;
- a system driven by **external electrical control**;
- behavior fully fixed by a **foreign purpose** programmed in from outside;
- a **purely mechanistic** explanation that leaves nothing over.

The last defeater is the sharp one, and it loops back to §4: **if we fully understand the mechanism, is the agency still there?** A puppet is not an agent because its purposes are someone else's. The unsettling question for AI is whether a model trained to optimize an externally specified objective is, in the relevant sense, a very sophisticated puppet.

---

## 8. The skeptical view: determinism and the "view from nowhere"

**Determinism** is the position that only one future is physically possible — that apparent choices are, at bottom, illusions. If true, it threatens feature 2 (the open future) directly.

Thomas Nagel's **"view from nowhere"** names the resulting bind. Zoom out to the impersonal scientific standpoint and agency seems to vanish: there are only events under laws. Yet from *inside* a life, we cannot stop treating ourselves and others as agents who deliberate and decide — and we could not function if we did. The two standpoints don't reconcile cleanly; agency lives in the gap between the lived view and the physical one.

---

## 9. Back to AI and LLMs

The lecture's payoff for the course is a careful "not yet, and maybe not in kind":

- An LLM **optimizes outputs** against an objective, but optimization is not obviously the same as genuine goal-directed *agency* — recall the puppet defeater (§7).
- **Projective Simulation** (Briegel and collaborators) is offered as one *quantitative* model of decision-making and agency — an attempt to make "deliberation" formal rather than metaphorical, and a reminder that "agency" need not stay purely philosophical.
- The features in §6 give a checklist rather than a verdict: today's models clear some bars (goals, learning history) and stumble on others (an open future, animal-style embodiment).

**Question for class.** During the lecture one question was raised but left open: *is the "agency" of AI products an explicit corporate strategy?* When a company markets a chatbot as an "agent" that "decides" and "wants," is it making a careful ontological claim, or deploying agency-language because it sells — and are firms ever explicit about which? Tie this back to §3: marketing borrows the *thin* sense of agency (it acts) to imply the *thick* one (it is the kind of thing you can trust, or hold responsible). Which sense is doing the persuasive work?

---

## 10. Core takeaway

Agency sits in the gap between everyday human experience and scientific explanation. The open challenge — the one the whole lecture circles — is whether agency is **genuinely real**, or only a useful description layered on top of physics. For this course the question is not idle: every claim we make about "agentic" AI, augmented democracy, or model misalignment inherits whichever answer we give.
