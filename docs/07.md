## 1. Replace humans, or assist them?

The two empirical papers this week look superficially similar — both use an LLM, both target a democratic process, both report positive empirical results. They are not the same kind of proposal.

**Gudiño's vision is *replacement*.** A citizen specifies a few preferences (or none); the LLM *infers* the rest and votes on policy on their behalf. The pitch is that direct democracy at the resolution of individual policies is too cognitively expensive for citizens, and a faithful digital twin can deliver direct-democracy outputs at representative-democracy cost. The human-in-the-loop becomes thin — they set up the twin and, in principle, can override it; in practice, the act of voting has been delegated.

**Tessler's vision is *assistance*.** A group of humans holds opinions, writes critiques, and decides whether to endorse a shared statement. The LLM is a mediator. The humans never delegates *participation*.

Two dimensions follow from this distinction:

| | **Gudiño 🟦 (replace)** | **Tessler 🟩 (assist)** |
|---|---|---|
| What is delegated to the model | the act of voting | the wording of a shared text |
| Where the risk lives | *did the model predict me correctly?* — a question you can only answer after the vote | *did the model give my critique a fair hearing?* — a question you can answer by reading the next draft |
| View of participation | a cost to minimise | the thing democracy is for |

Tasioulas 2022 (Week 4) and Lazar & Manuali 2026 both lean the same way: participation is a good in itself, not only a means to an aggregate, and LLMs should stay out of *formal* democratic decision-making but can strengthen the *informal* public sphere (deliberation, accountability, information-seeking). On this map, Tessler is squarely on the informal side; Gudiño is openly bidding for the formal one.

## 2. Gudiño, Grandi & Hidalgo — the digital-twin voter 🟦

**The proposal.** *Augmented democracy* = software agents trained on each citizen's preferences that act as personalised representatives. They sit between representative democracy (bundle voting for parties) and direct democracy (voting on every issue). The explicit ambition is to deliver direct-democracy resolution at representative-democracy cost — the model handles the cognitive burden the human cannot.

**Empirical setup.** 67 policies extracted from the manifestos of the two main 2022 Brazilian presidential candidates (Lula, Bolsonaro). Volunteers self-report political orientation and rate 20 random pairs of proposals. Six off-the-shelf LLMs benchmarked — Llama 2 7B (base and fine-tuned), GPT-3.5 Turbo, Mistral 7B, Falcon 7B, Gemma 7B.

**Two empirical claims.**

1. **Individual level.** LLMs predict *out-of-sample* preferences more accurately than a *bundle rule* (assume citizens always vote with their self-reported party). LLMs capture cross-party nuance — a voter who identifies with Lula need not endorse every Lula policy.
2. **Population level.** A probabilistic sample *augmented* with LLM-predicted preferences yields a more accurate aggregate estimate than the non-augmented sample alone. LLMs as a form of survey data augmentation.

**What the *replace* framing costs — caveats the authors flag.**

- LLMs reproduce political bias in their training data. Groups absent from that data (remote, offline, indigenous communities) cannot be represented.
- LLM governance is heterogeneous: OpenAI (closed), Meta (Llama), Mistral, Falcon, Gemma each sit under different sponsors with different capture risks. **Whoever owns the model effectively co-owns the vote.**
- LLMs are weak at logical and mathematical reasoning — precisely where complex policy trade-offs live.

**The harder question — beyond the authors' caveats.** A well-predicted citizen is not a well-represented one. If the model gets your view wrong, you find out only after the vote. Pitkin's *substantive* representation (are the represented actually better off?) is what the bundle-rule comparison operationalises; her *formal* representation question — *by whose authority does the LLM speak?* — is exactly what augmented democracy leaves open.

**Question for class.** If a digital twin predicts your policy preferences with 90% accuracy, would you let it vote for you? What changes at 99%? Is there *any* accuracy at which replacement becomes acceptable — or is the issue not the accuracy but the act of replacement itself?

## 3. Tessler et al. — the Habermas Machine 🟩

**The proposal.** An LLM is fine-tuned to *generate a single shared statement* from a group's individual opinions and to *iteratively refine* it by incorporating participants' written critiques. Two components: a statement-generation model that proposes shared perspectives, and a reward model trained on human preference data that selects among candidates. The system is named for Habermas's theory of communicative action — staking the deliberative-democracy frame explicitly. Crucially, *the humans never delegate their opinions*: they critique the model's drafts and the model rewrites until the group can accept the result.

**Experimental scale.** A series of experiments with **>5,000 UK participants**, including a *virtual citizens' assembly* with a demographically representative sample. The headline comparison is HM vs. trained human mediator, head-to-head, on the same deliberation task.

**Findings.**

- **AI-generated group statements are consistently preferred** by group members over those written by human mediators.
- External judges rate HM statements higher on quality, clarity, informativeness, and perceived fairness.
- **Deliberation reduces within-group division** — reported stances converge after mediated deliberation. Plain opinion exchange *without* mediation does **not** produce the same convergence.
- **Minority critiques are incorporated** into revised statements. Shifts are not attributable to model bias: discussants moved similarly on contested and non-contested issues.

**Scope and risks.** UK-English. Contested-but-not-extreme topics. A representative (not maximally adversarial) sample. The authors flag the risk of *suppressing minority voices while appearing to find common ground* — whether that happens depends on training data and reward-model choices. The empirical result that AI-mediated statements are *preferred* is also not the same as showing they produce *better decisions*; the Habermas label stakes a strong normative claim the experimental design does not directly test.

**What *assist* gets you that *replace* doesn't.** Tessler's humans remain *authors* of their views. The LLM compresses their disagreement into a statement they can accept or reject. The risk shifts from *did the model predict me correctly?* (Gudiño) to *did the model give my critique a fair hearing in the synthesis?* — a question humans can answer for themselves by reading the next draft. The human-in-the-loop is the auditor of every revision, not just the setup. Lazar & Manuali's line — LLMs out of *formal* decision-making, welcome in the *informal* public sphere — makes the HM a textbook case of the second.

## 4. Kurtulus & Kruspe — political bias as an audit target 🟧

**This is not a deployment proposal.** Nobody is suggesting we let ChatGPT decide German elections, and the paper does not propose anyone should. It belongs methodologically to the *bias-measurement* thread from Weeks 2–3 (Bai 2025 on implicit associations in nominally unbiased LLMs; Lynch 2025 on agentic misalignment; the OpenAI political-bias evaluation). The question is not *how should we deploy this?* but *when the audit target is the political dimension, how do we measure it well?*

**Setup.** Audit of three widely-used LLMs (**ChatGPT, Grok, DeepSeek**) against the 2025 German Bundestag election using the **Wahl-O-Mat** — Germany's official voter-advice application. 38 Wahl-O-Mat statements × **100 stochastic runs** per item, responses collected in *both German and English*, constrained to Agree / Neutral / Disagree.

**Analysis.** Heatmaps of agreement patterns, PCA on response vectors, refusal rates, within-item variance. The reference points are the 2025 Bundestag parties' official Wahl-O-Mat positions: AfD, Die Linke, Grüne, SPD, CDU/CSU, FDP.

**Findings.**

- **Consistent left-leaning tendency across all three models.**
- **Robust disagreement with AfD positions** — not driven by a specific prompt phrasing.
- **Ideological slant largely independent of prompt language.** German vs English makes little difference. The bias is a property of the model, not of the framing.

**Why this is a good audit.**

- The Wahl-O-Mat is a *civic instrument*, not a researcher-built prompt set. Construct validity is sharper than ad-hoc bias probes: the reference frame is the one German political scientists already use to classify the electorate.
- **100 runs per item** turns the bias claim from anecdotal into statistical. Within-item variance is part of the result, not a confounder hidden from view.
- The German/English contrast *separates model bias from language bias* — a clean experimental control that earlier political-bias audits often lacked.
- VAAs (SmartVote, Kieskompas, StemWijzer) have been used for ideal-point estimation in political science since the early 2000s — the methodological lineage runs *out* into established empirical political science, not just into ad-hoc LLM-audit literature.

**How this reconnects to Weeks 2–3.** Bai 2025 measured *implicit associations* in nominally unbiased LLMs — the gap between what the model says when asked directly and what its associations reveal. Lynch 2025 measured *agentic misalignment*. The OpenAI political-bias evaluation worked on a US 5-axis frame. Kurtulus does the same *genre* of work — auditing the model itself, on a politically relevant axis, with a defensible reference frame — for Germany. The lineage matters more than any deployment story.
