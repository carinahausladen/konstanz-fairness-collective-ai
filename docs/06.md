The thread this week: **AI's economic impact is, in this course, a fairness question. Who counts as a stakeholder, what gets measured, and which harms appear in the data are *normative* choices made before any policy debate begins.** Acemoglu names the mechanisms. Teutloff and Massenkoff race two different methods at the same target — displacement. Ranjit drags an invisible dimension — meaning — into the conversation. Across all four, the same pattern: *the choice of measurement frame is the choice of whose harm becomes visible.*

---

## 1. Acemoglu — the mechanisms of harm 🟦

Before we measure displacement we need a theory of *why* it happens. Acemoglu's *Harms of AI* (Oxford Handbook of AI Governance, working paper, August 2021) is the conceptual scaffolding for the rest of the week.

**Claim.** Harms are **not inherent to AI technology** — they flow from how AI is developed and deployed to empower firms and governments against workers and citizens. The technology is endogenous to a political economy.

**Three harm domains.**

- **Data and information.** Privacy violation (firms extract consumer surplus via price discrimination), unfair (data-advantaged) competition, behavioral manipulation that exploits biases consumers themselves cannot recognize.
- **Labor market — four channels.** *Excessive automation* (firms don't internalize the negative externalities they impose on workers); *distorted composition of technology* (automation is favored over new-task creation); *loss of economies of scope in human judgment* (handing decisions to machines erodes human decision quality); *worker monitoring* (surveillance is attractive to firms but socially inefficient).
- **Communication and democracy.** Echo chambers from AI-curated feeds; Big-Brother surveillance effects; a political-economic channel — automation weakens workers' bargaining power by making them less indispensable.

**A surprising implication.** Greater *competition* can **exacerbate** these harms rather than resolve them, because the harms ride on the underlying use of AI to redirect surplus from workers to capital. Regulation and the redirection of AI research are part of the remedy.

**Fairness angle.** Acemoglu's four labor channels are the empirically usable hypotheses behind every reading that follows. Each subsequent paper can be read as testing one channel from a different angle: Teutloff = excessive automation in a freelance market; Massenkoff = the demographic shape of distorted technology composition; Ranjit = loss of economies of scope where the *valued* parts of a job, not the wage, are what gets automated away.

## 2. Teutloff et al. — causal evidence on freelance markets 🟩

If Acemoglu names mechanisms, Teutloff measures one of them with a sharp pre/post boundary in a real labor market.

**Design.** BERTopic clustering of postings on a major online freelance platform → 116 fine-grained skill clusters. GPT-4o classifies each cluster as *substitutable*, *complementary*, or *unaffected* by LLMs. Difference-in-differences around the ChatGPT launch (30 Nov 2022).

**Findings.**

- **Substitutable skills fall 20–50%** relative to counterfactual trend.
- Writing and translation worst hit: Real-Estate / About-Us writing **−52%**, Western European translation **−59%**.
- **Complementary skills +7% on average, but highly heterogeneous**: ML programming **+24%**, AI chatbot development **+179%**, Machine Learning +37%.
- **Sharpest decline for short-term (1–3 week) jobs** — the entry points novices typically use.
- **Novice workers lose out.** The "LLMs help novices" finding from lab studies (Brynjolfsson & McAfee; Noy & Zhang) does *not* generalize to freelance markets.

**Fairness angle.** The heterogeneity is the point. "Winners and losers" is *not visible at the occupation level* — "writers" or "developers" hides the action. It's visible at the skill-cluster level, where the gap between AI-chatbot devs (+179%) and translators (−59%) is two orders of magnitude inside the same labor market. The choice of measurement granularity is itself a fairness move: aggregating away the skill cluster is choosing to hide the losers.

## 3. Massenkoff & McCrory — observed exposure 🟧

Massenkoff is methodological as much as empirical: how do we even *measure* AI exposure?

**Three measurement families racing each other in two years.**

- **Pure capability / feasibility** — Eloundou et al. 2023, *GPTs are GPTs*. What an LLM *could* do. Strong upper bound; weak predictor of what actually happens.
- **Provider telemetry** — Anthropic Economic Index v3, v4, India Brief. What people *actually do* with the model, from anonymized conversation logs. Large, real, recent — but one provider, skewed toward early adopters.
- **Capability × observed usage** — Massenkoff's contribution. Weight Eloundou-style feasibility by how much of each task is actually automated (vs augmented), then aggregate to occupations.

**Findings under the observed-exposure measure.**

- **Tasks with Eloundou β = 1 (fully feasible) account for 68% of observed Claude usage; β = 0 tasks only 3%.** Actual coverage is a fraction of what's theoretically possible, but concentrated on the most-exposed tasks.
- Higher-exposure occupations are projected by BLS to **grow less** through 2034.
- Most-exposed workers are **older, more female, more educated, higher-paid** — flips the classic "low-skill automation" framing.
- **No systematic unemployment increase** for highly-exposed workers since late 2022. Suggestive evidence that **hiring of younger workers has slowed** in exposed occupations — converges with Teutloff's novice effect from a different method.

**Fairness angle.** The measure *is* the politics. Pure feasibility (Eloundou) gives **96% theoretical** AI coverage of Computer & Math work — an apocalyptic number that hasn't materialized. Observed exposure tempers it to **32%**. Choosing between the two changes who appears displaced, who appears safe, and what policy follows. Massenkoff is the cleanest example in the syllabus of *measurement choice as fairness choice*.

## 4. Ranjit et al. — the meaning of work 🟥

Wages and employment are the easy variables. They are not the only fairness-relevant ones.

**Setup.** 171 tasks across 22 occupations sampled from O*NET. *Two surveys, asking different questions of different people.*

- **Survey 1 — meaningfulness.** **202 workers** (only) rate the 171 tasks on five dimensions of meaningful work: perceived bullsh*t, perceived value, well-being, status maintenance, human flourishing.
- **Survey 2 — desired AI traits.** **202 workers + 197 developers** rate, for the same 171 tasks, what psychological traits an AI system *should* have when augmenting the task (12 trait pairs: polite vs straightforward, imaginative vs practical, …).
- **LM scaling.** LM-as-expert prompting then extends coverage to **10,131 tasks across 512 occupations**, validated against the human ratings (moderate-to-high intra-class correlation).

**Findings.**

- **Survey 1 — AI exposure is negatively correlated with meaningfulness.** Tasks AI does well tend to be emotionally thin, transactional, lacking in-person interaction, low on relationships and social connection.
- **Survey 2 — developer–worker design gap.** Builders prefer *polite, strict, imaginative* AI systems; workers prefer *straightforward, tolerant, practical* ones — a concrete, measured value mismatch between who designs AI and who lives with it.

**Fairness angle.** Two fairness questions at once. *Procedurally,* the developer–worker gap is a measurable representation problem inside the design process: the people building these systems have systematically different preferences from the people whose jobs they reshape. *Substantively,* automation can displace the *valued* parts of a job without displacing the job — the wage stays, the meaning leaves. Pure wage/employment statistics miss this entirely. Ranjit is the variable that economic readings systematically exclude.
