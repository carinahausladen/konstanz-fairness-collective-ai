The thread this week: **Week 4 framed alignment as social choice *in principle*. This week the rule meets deployment — where the aggregation rule itself shapes the alternatives it ranks, the inputs it receives, and the participants who show up.** Each of this week's three papers identifies a failure mode that classical social-choice theory *predicts* once you take political economy and the sociology of collective action seriously.

## 2. Donahue & Raghavan — leaderboards as voting rules over producers 🟦

In classical social choice, the alternatives to be ranked are **given**. In an AI marketplace they are *produced* by strategic actors who respond to whatever rule ranks them. The same scoring function does double duty: it ranks model outputs *and* it shapes which models get built.

**The paper's claim.** Modern AI leaderboards (LMArena-style benchmarks, routers, model marketplaces) score models on **winrate** — the fraction of pairwise comparisons each model wins. Donahue & Raghavan model this as a game between model producers and prove two things:

- Under winrate, the equilibrium is **homogenisation**: producers converge on the modal-preferred answer, and consumers with niche preferences lose welfare.
- A different scoring rule — **weighted winrate**, which credits a model for prompts where it has comparative advantage — *provably* increases consumer welfare and rewards specialisation.

**The social-choice angle.** A leaderboard is an aggregation rule over a slate of producers, exactly as a voting rule is an aggregation rule over a slate of candidates. Like every aggregation rule it is normative, not technical: the choice of scoring function is the choice of *which fairness desideratum to drop*. Winrate drops the welfare of niche-preference users; weighted winrate keeps it but pays the price elsewhere (more interpretability complexity, more potential for gaming).

**Bridge to last week.** Zhang's algorithmic-monoculture finding — 21 LLMs aligned with only 41 % of human preferences — was framed as a *demand-side* problem (annotators are too similar). Donahue & Raghavan give it a *supply-side* reading: under the wrong scoring rule, producers will converge regardless of who annotates.

## 3. Chuai et al. — bridging-based ranking under deployment 🟩

Most voting rules are **majoritarian**: surface what the majority prefers. X's Community Notes uses an explicitly **non-majoritarian** rule — and Chuai et al. measure how that rule behaves once it is deployed live.

**The bridging algorithm.** A matrix factorisation on rating data assigns each rater $u$ and each note $n$ two latent scalars:

- **polarity** $f_u, f_n$ — a left–right-style axis,
- **helpfulness intercept** $i_u, i_n$ — an axis-orthogonal baseline.

A note is displayed only if its $i_n$ exceeds a threshold. Crucially, $i_n$ is high only when raters from **both poles of the polarity axis** call the note helpful. Approval from one pole alone → not displayed. The rule deliberately down-weights one-sided support; it encodes a *procedural* value (cross-partisan agreement) rather than an *outcome* value.

**The paper's finding.** Across 437k notes and 35M ratings, **30.2 % of notes that are displayed as helpful later lose that status**. Mechanism: the early rater pool — the contributors who find a note before display — is bridging-oriented. Once display pushes the note to a much larger and more polarised audience, the new rater distribution drags the helpfulness intercept back below threshold.

**The social-choice angle.** Classical aggregation theory assumes preferences are **exogenous to the rule**: same voters, same views, regardless of what the rule outputs. In a deployed system, displaying the rule's output *changes who shows up to rate*, and how. The aggregation is only as stable as the input distribution it operates on — and deployment changes that distribution. This is a problem the theory ignores; Chuai et al. document it empirically at scale on the largest open algorithmic-aggregation system we have.

## 4. Nudo et al. — who actually shows up to vote 🟧

Every classical social-choice theorem implicitly assumes **universal participation**: every voter votes. On a volunteer platform, participation is heavy-tailed and the theorems break.

**The paper's finding.** Community Notes rating activity follows a power law: a small minority of contributors generates the bulk of the ratings, and that minority is **politically selective** — its distribution of preferences is not representative of the broader rater pool. Small perturbations to the hyperactive minority produce markedly different system outcomes.

**The social-choice angle.** The "majority rule" of any aggregator is the majority *of the participating subset* — not the majority of the population. Bridging-based ranking presumes the rater pool spans the polarity axis evenly; heavy-tailed participation breaks that presumption silently. PRISM and Community Alignment fixed *who is invited* to rate; Nudo et al. show that even with an appropriately diverse invited population, **the actual rater pool is the subset that shows up**, and that subset can be small, polarised, and decisive.

## 5. Three claims to leave the room with

1. **Aggregation shapes supply, not just demand.** Leaderboards and routers are voting rules over producers; their scoring choices determine whether the model population converges or diversifies (Donahue & Raghavan).
2. **Deployed rules perturb their own inputs.** Display changes who rates and how. Static voting theorems assume preferences are exogenous to the rule, and break under feedback (Chuai et al.).
3. **The "community" is a minority.** Universal participation is a theoretical idealisation; real volunteer platforms run on a heavy-tailed, politically-selective subset — and the bridging property the algorithm encodes can be undone by the participation distribution alone (Nudo et al.).
