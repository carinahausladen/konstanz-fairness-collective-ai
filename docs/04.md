The thread this week: **what RLHF silently does to the question of "whose values" — and why that question is, formally, a social-choice problem.**

---

## 1. The normative starting point — alignment with *whose* values?

Before any technique, the alignment program inherits an unresolved question from political philosophy: an AI system can be aligned to many different targets, and the choice among them is normative, not technical.

**🟦 Gabriel** lays out a ladder of six increasingly demanding targets:

1. **Instructions** — literal task commands.
2. **Expressed intentions** — what the user actually wanted.
3. **Revealed preferences** — what behaviour implies about desires.
4. **Informed preferences / desires** — what a fully-informed version of the user would want.
5. **Interest / well-being** — what is objectively good for the user.
6. **Values** — what the user *should* value, given moral considerations.

Each rung subsumes the previous and demands more interpretive, normative work from the system designer. Climbing the ladder is not a technical refinement — it is a step toward harder questions about *whose* preferences count, *who* aggregates them, *under what procedure*, *accountable to whom*.

This is where the social-science backbone of the week starts: alignment is not a property of a model in isolation; it is a property of a *procedure* that takes many human inputs and yields one model's behaviour.

---

## 2. Social choice — the formal machinery for "many → one"

The discipline that has studied *aggregate inconsistent, irrational, heterogeneous preferences into a collective decision* for seventy years is **social choice theory**. Three building blocks worth naming explicitly:

**2.1 Arrow's impossibility theorem (1951).** No aggregation rule can simultaneously satisfy a small set of seemingly mild fairness conditions — unrestricted domain, non-dictatorship, Pareto efficiency, independence of irrelevant alternatives. Every voting rule must trade off at least one. *There is no neutral aggregation.*

**2.2 Pairwise vs cardinal methods.** Classical aggregation rules — Borda (sum of ranks), Condorcet / Kemeny–Young (majority pairwise winner), Bradley–Terry–Luce (probabilistic model over pairwise comparisons) — pick different winners on the same ballot set. The choice of method is itself a normative act.

**2.3 The aggregation step is load-bearing.** Whatever you pick, you have implicitly chosen which fairness desideratum to drop and which subgroup will be silenced when preferences conflict. The aggregation is not a neutral pre-processing step; it is the place where the political question lives.

**🟩 Conitzer et al.** make the bridge to LLMs explicit: **RLHF is a voting rule in disguise.** The standard pipeline:

- collects pairwise preference comparisons,
- fits a Bradley–Terry–Luce reward model,
- optimizes the policy against that reward.

That is one specific aggregation rule (BTL over pairwise data), with all the trade-offs Arrow guarantees. Mishra (2023) applies Arrow's theorem directly to RLHF — formally treating the reward-modelling step as a social welfare function and inheriting the impossibility consequences. Constitutional AI doesn't escape either; it just pushes the aggregation problem one level up (how is the constitution itself written, and how are its principles aggregated?).

The Conitzer / Gabriel pairing is the spine of the week. Gabriel sets up the normative question; Conitzer shows the formal machinery already exists to address it, and that current RLHF silently chooses one specific answer.

## 3. From theory to data — what does heterogeneous human feedback actually look like?

Once we accept that aggregation matters, the next question is empirical: how heterogeneous *are* human preferences over LLM outputs, and can current alignment datasets capture that heterogeneity?

**🟧 Kirk — PRISM.** The first alignment dataset designed specifically to *expose* rather than average away cross-cultural and interpersonal disagreement. The three design moves give the acronym:

- **Participatory** — every participant writes their own personal "system string" (a personal constitution) before interacting.
- **Representative** — census-representative samples in the UK and US.
- **Individualised** — every rating is linked to a detailed participant profile, enabling per-person preference modelling.

Scale: 1,500 participants, 75 countries, 21 LLMs, 8,011 conversations. The dataset deliberately weights values-guided (30.7%) and controversy-guided (30.4%) prompts alongside an unguided baseline (38.9%) — concentrating effort exactly where preferences should diverge.

Three demonstrated case studies in the paper:

1. Different demographics initiate different discussions.
2. Different people prefer different aligned models.
3. Sampling decisions affect welfare outcomes.

**🟥 Zhang — Community Alignment.** Two-part study, multilingual throughout (English, French, Italian, Portuguese, Hindi; 5 countries: US, France, Italy, Brazil, India).

- A nationally-representative **survey** (N = 15,000) demonstrating that human preferences vary substantially across cultures while all 21 evaluated LLMs cluster tightly together.
- A new **preference dataset** — *Community Alignment* itself — with 233,319 comparisons from 3,603 unique annotators, built using the sampling technique below.

Two contributions beyond PRISM:

- **Empirical demonstration of algorithmic monoculture.** On the Inglehart–Welzel cultural map, humans vary substantially across cultures; all 21 evaluated LLMs cluster tightly together and align with only **41%** of human preferences globally.
- **Negatively-correlated (NC) sampling.** Preference datasets are limited not only by *who annotates*, but by *which candidate responses* the annotator gets to rate. If the candidates are themselves homogeneous, no annotator-side diversity can recover heterogeneous values. Zhang et al. propose generating candidates intentionally spread along value dimensions, so the resulting preferences carry information about value variation rather than stylistic preference.

---

## 4. The dual sources of monoculture

Putting Kirk and Zhang together yields the cleanest empirical lesson of the week: **alignment monoculture has two distinct sources**, and a dataset that fixes only one is insufficient.

| Source                    | Mechanism                                                                                                                     | Addressed by                    |
| ------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------- |
| **Annotator homogeneity** | Feedback comes from a narrow (Western, English, educated) population                                                          | PRISM, Community Alignment      |
| **Candidate homogeneity** | Models being compared all generate from the same training distribution → the rater is choosing among near-identical responses | Community Alignment NC sampling |

Conitzer's social-choice framing predicts this: a voting rule operates on *ballots*, and a ballot is a function of both the voters and the alternatives presented to them. If either is impoverished, the aggregate is impoverished.

## 6. Three claims to leave the room with

1. **There is no neutral aggregation.** Every RLHF / DPO / CAI pipeline embeds a specific voting rule with specific Arrow-style trade-offs, whether or not its designers intended that.
2. **"Whose values" is not rhetorical.** Gabriel's ladder shows the question is built in to the alignment target itself, and the answer requires political philosophy, not just engineering.
3. **Pluralistic alignment needs both pluralistic annotators and pluralistic candidates.** PRISM addresses the first axis; Community Alignment shows the second is independent and equally load-bearing.
